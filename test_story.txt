The lighthouse keeper climbed the spiral staircase as the storm rolled in from the west. Rain lashed against the windows, and the old stone walls trembled with each gust of wind. At the top, she wiped the salt spray from the glass and lit the great lamp, watching its beam cut through the darkness like a golden sword.

Below, a small fishing boat struggled against the waves. Its single sail was torn, flapping uselessly in the gale. The keeper grabbed her signal flags and rushed to the gallery, waving frantically — directing the boat toward the sheltered cove on the island's eastern shore.

The fisherman saw the flags. He hauled on the tiller with blistered hands, turning the bow toward safety. The boat rode a massive wave over the reef and slid into the calm waters of the cove, scraping against the sandy bottom. He collapsed over the gunwale, breathing hard, alive.

At dawn, the keeper found him asleep on the beach. She brought him bread and hot tea from the lighthouse kitchen. They sat together on the rocks, watching the storm retreat eastward, leaving behind a sky washed clean and impossibly blue. Neither spoke for a long time. The silence between them was enough.
